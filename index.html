The Ocean

Inspired by "The deep Sea" by Neal Agarwal

Epipelagic Zone (0-200 meters)

This layer is also called the sunlight zone and extends from the surface to 200 meters.
In this zone is where most of the visible light exists and with that comes heat - sea surface temperatures range from as high as 36°C to -2°C.
The base of this layer is called the thermocline. Dolphins and Tuna: Many large fish and marine mammals, like dolphins and tuna, thrive in this zone.

Mesopelagic Zone (200-1,000 meters)

Sometimes referred to as the twilight zone or the midwater zone, as sunlight is very faint.
Because of the lack of light, bioluminescence begins to appear on organisms in this zone.
The eyes on the fishes are also larger and generally upward directed, most likely to see silhouettes of other animals (for food) against the dim light.

Bathypelagic Zone (1,000-4,000 meters)

Due to its constant darkness, this zone is also called the midnight zone.
The only light at this depth and lower comes from the bioluminescence of the animals themselves.
The temperature in the bathypelagic zone, unlike that of the mesopelagic zone, is constant.
The temperature never fluctuates far from a chilling 4°C.
The pressure in the bathypelagic zone is extreme and at depths of 4,000m, reaches over 5850 pounds per square inch.
Yet, sperm whales can dive down to this level in search of food.

Abyssopelagic Zone (4,000-6,000 meters)

This is the pitch-black bottom layer of the ocean.
Water temperature is constantly near freezing, and only a few creatures can be found at these crushing depths.
Three quarters of the ocean floor lies in this zone.
Challenger Deep: The deepest known point in the ocean, located in the Mariana Trench, reaches about 10,994 meters.

Hadalpelagic Zone (6,000-11,000 meters)

The deepest zone of the ocean, the temperature is constant at just above freezing.
The weight of all the water overhead in the Mariana Trench is over 8 tonnes per square inch.
Even at the very bottom, life still exists. In 2005, tiny single-celled organisms called foraminifera, a type of plankton were discovered in the Challenger Deep trench southwest of Guam.
The deepest fish ever to have been filmed, Pseudoliparis snailfish, was found 8336m down in a trench off the coast of Japan.
Ocean Trenches: This zone includes the deepest parts of the ocean, such as the Mariana Trench.
Challenger Deep: The deepest known point in the ocean, located in the Mariana Trench, reaches about 10,994 meters.
